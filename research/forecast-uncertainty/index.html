Polls, Forecasts, and Voters’ Perceptions of Uncertainty

An experiment about uncertainty over polls and forecasts (with Benjamin Toff and Zach Warner)

Last updated on Jan 1, 2020
Paper Open Source

This paper uses a conjoint design to examine the relationship between common journalistic practices around the presentation of polling data and the degree to which news audiences may become overconfident that candidates are likely to win or lose hypothetical elections. The survey experiment, conducted on a sample drawn from Mechanical Turk (MTurk), tests how people process polling data when it is expressed as a single result, as an average of recent polls, or as a probabilistic forecast, across a variety of vote share conditions and levels of undecided voters. Journalistic strategies thought to mitigate tendencies toward overconfidence and misinterpretation are also examined, including language about polls’ margins of sampling error and visualizations illustrating ranges of possible outcomes. Moderating factors including numeracy and education will also be examined. Our results help to empirically ground discussions about ways to improve coverage of survey data and public opinion by accounting for how the public actually understands and makes sense of such numbers.
