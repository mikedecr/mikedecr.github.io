"Operationalizations Aren't Definitions" and Random Variables

With an Application to Racial Resentment

Michael DeCrescenzo
Jul 30, 2019 3 min read Methods

Motivation

Paul Musgrave tweeted a while back that operationalizations aren’t definitions.

Operationalizations aren’t definitions

— Paul Musgrave (@profmusgrave) May 6, 2019

What’s this all about? When we do (social) science, our theories involve “forces at work in the world.” We often think and talk about these forces with some fuzziness, but in order to systematically analyze the world, we need to be careful about what we’re talking about and how we know that we’re properly observing it. This applies equally to quantitative or qualitative work.

When we are being appropriately careful, we provide definitions for important theoretical constructs. These definitions are conceptual: what are we talking about, what are we not talking about, and so on. We also, as a separate part of the work, operationalize a concept. Operationalization the researcher’s strategy for observing the construct. “I’m using [some such observable phenomenon] as an indicator of the concept I’m interested in.”

The purpose of this post is to explore a statistical argument about “operationalizations,” how we ought to be far more uncertain about them. Others have certainly made arguments like this before (and I’d be interested in citations to related work about this). What will maybe be distinctive about this piece is that I will use “racial resentment” as a working example throughout.

Measures of an unknown truth

\[\begin{align} \text{X}_{i} &= \text{Truth}_{i} + \text{Bias}_{i} + \text{Error}_{i} \end{align}\]

Bias: conceptual or operational bias. We’re looking in the wrong place even if we could perfectly observe what we wanted.

Error: measurement imprecision. Even if we’re looking in the right place, the process of measurement is distinct from definition and operationalization.

\(X_{i}\) as a random draw from a probability distribution of measurements with location \(\mu\) and dispersion \(\sigma\). \[\begin{align} \text{X}_{i} &\sim \mathcal{D}\left(\mu = \text{Truth}_{i} + \text{Bias}_{i}, \sigma = \text{Error}_{i}\right) \end{align}\]

Racial resentment as a latent variable

We have a sample of \(n\) respondents indexed \(i \in \{1, 2, \ldots, n\}\) and racial resentment battery items indexed \(j \in \{ 1, 2, \ldots, J \}\), where \(J\) typically equals 4.

Suppose \(x_{ij}\) is respondent \(i\)’s response to item \(j\).

\[\begin{align} x_{ij} &= \Phi\left( \beta_{j}\left[\theta - \kappa_{j}\right]\right) \\[12pt] x_{ij} &= \Phi\left( \beta_{j}\theta + \alpha_{j}\right), \text{ where } \alpha_{j} = -\beta_j\kappa_j \end{align}\]

\[\begin{align} \theta_{i} &= \frac{1}{J} \sum\limits_{j=1}^{J} x_{ij} \tag{1} \end{align}\]

What assumptions get us to this…

\[\begin{align} \theta_{i} + \epsilon_{ij} &= x_{ij} \tag{2} \end{align}\]

Where we can assume that \[\begin{align} \sum_{j = 1}^{J} \epsilon_{ij} &= 0 \text{ for all } i \end{align}\]

We get back to the original by summing responses over \(j\) \[\begin{align} \theta_{i} + \epsilon_{ij} &= x_{ij} \\[12pt] \sum\limits_{j=1}^{J} \left(\theta_{i} + \epsilon_{ij}\right) &= \sum\limits_{j=1}^{J} x_{ij} \\[12pt] \left(J \times \theta_{i}\right) + 0 &= \sum\limits_{j=1}^{J} x_{ij} \\[12pt] \theta_{i} &= \frac{1}{J} \sum\limits_{j=1}^{J} x_{ij} \end{align}\]

IRT reverse-engineering

\[\begin{align} x_{ij} &= \beta_{j}\left(\theta_{i} - \kappa_{j}\right) + \epsilon_{ij} \\ x_{ij} &= \beta_{j}\theta_{i} + \alpha_{j} + \epsilon_{ij} \end{align}\]

we only get Equation (2) when \(\alpha_{j} = 0\) and \(\beta_{j} = 1\) \[\begin{align} x_{ij} &= (1)\theta_{i} + (0) + \epsilon_{ij} \end{align}\]
