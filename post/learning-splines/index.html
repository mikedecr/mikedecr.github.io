What we’re doing

The more I learn about causal inference, the more I learn to appreciate the differences between the goals generative modeling vs. predictive modeling, as well as the trade-offs between interpretable models vs. accurate predictions.

Comment on Interpretability

I come at this from the point of view of academic political science, where a lot of applied work insists that there is a premium on interpretable methods.

I can think of two ways to define interpretability:

  1. simple enough that the researcher knows at a technical level why the model works for the use case, which is valuable for ensuring that the audience also knows how to understand the model.
  2. simple enough that the researcher can see, from the model output (coefficients) alone, the predictive influence of a single \(x\) on \(y\), which (spicy take) I am skeptical is in itself a virtue.

Elaborating: we want models that are appropriate for the task at hand, so the analyst needs to be able to understand why it would be appropriate, what its value added is over other potential model choices, and so on. If your audience is smart (you can assume that they are), your job is to be a good explainer of why the model works and where it can go wrong.

What I think is less important is to be able to directly eyeball one covariate’s linear effect on your conditional expectation of \(y\). For binary variables? Sure, if we know that there aren’t important interactions. But let’s think about the way that we model vs. what the “appropriate model” could possibly be.

Interpretability:

  • simple enough that I know how the model works and why it works, which can be good for ensuring that you and your audience knows what’s going on.
  • simple enough that I can “directly interpret” the predictive influence of \(x\) on \(y\), which is never a virtue in itself.
