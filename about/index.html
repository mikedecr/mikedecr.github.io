About Me

I grew up near St. Louis, MO, and studied political science the University of Missouri. I have spent some time in Berkeley, CA, taking summer courses and working in a nearby sandwich shop. I moved to Madison for graduate school in 2014.

In my free time, I am a fledgling cyclist and a jack-of-many-trades musician.

About my research

My research focuses on American electoral politics and statistical research methods.

I am interested in the performance and functioning of elections: how well elections represent citizen views, how they are administered, how citizens experience the task of voting. I have studied these topics with historical election returns, online experiments, targeted surveys, voting and registration records, and polling place reports.

My publications and current projects deal with voter identification requirements in the state of Wisconsin, the gender gap and other group dynamics in electoral coalitions, voters’ perceptions of uncertainty in election polls and forecasts, and Bayesian causal inference. My dissertation examines ideological representation in primary elections, developing new models to estimate the policy preferences of local partisan groups.

Methodologically, I explore and apply Bayesian methods for measurement and causal inference. I try to give each of my projects something methodologically distinctive—innovations on ideal point models, a bespoke Bayesian analysis for unconventional data, or new modeling approaches for old problems. I am also concerned with meta-research problems related to statistical and scientific inference, transparency and reproducibility, and publication bias.

About my teaching

Most of my teaching so far has focused on mathematical, statistical, and software foundations for social science research.

Most notably I have been instructor-of-record for statistical software courses for undergraduate and graduate students, and led graduate workshops on causal inference, reproducible workflows/typesetting, and math camp.

I have also served as a teaching assistant for courses about American political behavior, campaign finance, and survey research methods.
